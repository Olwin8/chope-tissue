Why We Chope with Tissue

The deliciously Singaporean story of seat‑reservation genius

Walk into any busy hawker centre at lunchtime and you'll see them: packs of tissue paper, sometimes a name card, occasionally an umbrella, all silently declaring "this seat is taken." The practice of choping (reserving) seats with personal items is so deeply embedded in Singapore’s food culture that it has become a national symbol – as recognisable as the Merlion itself. But how did the humble tissue packet become the ultimate seat reservation tool?

The Origins of the Chope

1970s–1980s

Hawker centres mushroom across Singapore. With limited seating during peak hours, people begin leaving personal belongings to secure spots while queuing for food. The tissue packet – cheap, ubiquitous, and given away freely as advertisements – emerges as the perfect "placeholder".

1990s

The tissue‑chope becomes a fully‑fledged social contract. A packet of tissues on a table says: "I’m coming back with my laksa. Please find another table." A mutual understanding forms: if you see a tissue, you respect it.

2000s

The Internet meme era begins. #ChopeCulture trends on local forums. Tourists marvel at the phenomenon, and the tissue packet becomes a quirky, uniquely Singaporean souvenir.

Today

ChopeTissue reinvents the tradition: premium, art‑inspired tissue packets that celebrate Singaporean identity. The chope is no longer just functional – it’s a fashion statement.

A tissue packet on a table is an unspoken promise: “I will return. This seat is taken. Please respect it.”

Why the Tissue, and Not Something Else?

Psychologists suggest that the tissue packet works because it is a territorial marker that is both visible and inherently temporary. Unlike a mobile phone or a wallet, a tissue packet signals that the owner will return shortly – it’s not valuable enough to be stolen, but distinctive enough to be noticed. A bag might imply forgetfulness; a tissue says “brb”.

Over time, the tissue packet also became a social equaliser. Whether you’re a CEO or a student, your packet of tissues holds the same power – a little square of paper can claim a prime spot at Maxwell Food Centre.

The Etiquette of the Chope

While choping is widely accepted, unwritten rules govern its use:

  • Don’t chope for too long. A tissue packet should not sit alone for more than 10 minutes. It’s a reservation, not a property deed.
  • Use only one item. Spreading multiple items across several seats is considered kiasu (overly competitive) and poor form.
  • Smile and say “sorry” if someone accidentally sits at your choped seat. Politeness makes the system work.
